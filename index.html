Welcome

The South Carolina Department of Transportation (SCDOT) widened U.S. Highway 17 between Jacksonboro and Garden’s Corner and replaced the 1947 Combahee River bridge. Archaeologists found six archaeological sites and seven architectural and landscape features along this section of roadway, and five submerged sites in the river near the bridge. The Combahee Ferry Historic District (CFHD) preserves these 18 important sites to recognize and honor people, events, activities, and places that are significant to the history of South Carolina and the United States.

Combahee River

From 1730 until 1920 the Combahee River was one of the state's most important rice growing regions.

Causeway

The original 1711 causeway was contructed half a mile upstream from its present location. The crossing moved to its present location between 1733 and 1735.

The U.S. Highway 17 Bridge

The first permanent bridge over the Combahee River was constructed in 1927; Harriet Tubman Memorial Bridge opened in 2008.

Charleston to Port Royal Road

A remnant of the Charleston to Port Royal Road parallels the western side of U.S. Highway 17 and follows an American Indian trading path.

Archaeological Site 38BU1216

Researchers found remnants of the ferry keeper’s house/tavern and other buildings associated with the operation of the Combahee Ferry.

Newport Plantation Rice Field flood control structure

This rice field structure is a restoration or replacement of an earlier structure used to control the flow of water in and out of a large canal.

Newport Plantation Rice Fields

Newport Plantation was owned by Henry Middleton II and included 1,520 acres worked by 224 slaves.

Cypress Plantation Rice Fields

Cypress plantation included 2,350 acres and was owned by William Heyward from 1836 until 1879.

Archaeological Site 38BU1217

This multiple-gun battery was constructed by Confederate soldiers around 1862 to defend the Charleston & Savannah Railroad.

Archaeological Site 38BU1884

This 800 foot long rifle trench protected the back side of the battery at 38BU1217.

Archaeological Site 38BU1885

This 600-foot-long rifle trench protected the back side of the battery at 38BU1217.

Archaeological Site 38CN255

Researchers found remnants of a wooden vessel they believe was a pontoon that supported a floating bridge over the Combahee River during the Civil War.

Archaeological Site 38CN257

These earthen embankments and ditches were constructed by Confederate forces during the Civil War to defend the Combahee Ferry crossing and the Charleston & Savannah Railroad.

Archaeological Site 38BU1218

This African American cemetery contains the graves of two veterans from the African American regiment that participated in the 1863 Combahee River raid.

Archaeological Site 38BU2137

This site is a scatter of bricks and old bottle glass fragments, along with pilings, timbers, and debris from the 1954 bridge.

Archaeological Site 38BU2138

This site is a scatter of bottle glass fragments, ballast stones, modern bridge construction debris, and riprap.

Archaeological Site 38CN19

This scatter of brick, ballast stone, pottery, and bottle glass is from the former ferry landing on the Colleton County bank of the river.

Archaeological Site 38CN256

This scatter of ballast stones and 18th bottle glass fragments may be the location of the eastern ferry landing.
