The marshes along the Combahee River were the setting of a vast rice empire from 1740 until 1863. Even today it is not hard to imagine these marshes as thousands of acres of golden rice. This landscape is one of the features that makes CFHD a significant place in South Carolina. The Combahee River rice plantations were owned by some of the richest men in South Carolina. Their affluent lifestyle is hard to image. Commercial rice production in South Carolina ended in 1920 and moved west to Louisiana and Texas.

"If you ask me what I am doing, I should tell you nothing. My father is a Rice planter and his Sons have enough money to live handsomely and at leisure."

—Edward Barnwell Heyward, 1855

Combahee River Rice Plantations

Henry Middleton (pictured below), William Heyward, Stephen Bull and others made great fortunes growing rice along the Combahee River with the labor of thousands of enslaved Africans. Henry Middleton’s Newport and Hobonny plantations extended from just below U.S. 17 as far upriver as the eye can see. William Heyward’s Cypress Plantation was located across the river. In 1850, 300 slaves at Cypress Plantation produced 1,350,000 pounds of rice. The marshes visible from the U.S. Highway 17 causeway were Cypress rice fields. In 1863, the Union Army struck at the heart of South Carolina’s plantation economy by burning Combahee River rice plantations.

    9 of the 10 richest men in British North America live in the Carolina Lowcountry.

  • 1850

    523 slaves on Henry Middleton's Combahee River plantations produce 2,812,000 pounds of rice — a crop worth around 2.5 million dollars in today's money.

  • 1863

    Union troops burn rice plantations along the Combahee during two days of raids.

Rice and Slavery

From the founding of Carolina, enslaved Africans were imported into the colony as agricultural workers. As rice production expanded, the number of African captives brought to the colony increased dramatically. Rice was grown in parts of Africa for hundreds of years before it became a Carolina commodity. Planters preferred people from Africa’s rice growing countries. Captives from these areas knew how to build fields, control water, and plant and process the rice.

Did you know?

  • Approximately 500,000 Africans were brought to North America before the direct importation of captives was banned in 1808.
  • Forty percent of the captives brought to the United States came through the port of Charleston.
  • In 1860, four million people were enslaved in the United States.
  • The 13th Amendment to the Constitution abolished slavery.

Building Rice Fields

Early on, enslaved people built fields and grew rice in swamps along creeks and small branches away from the main channel of the Combahee River. In the late eighteenth century, planters decided to move their fields closer to the river and use the tide to flood their fields. It is estimated that enslaved people moved more than six million cubic feet of earth in the Combahee River region to construct a complex network of rice fields, canals, dikes, and banks. Today the former rice fields are covered in large stands of marsh grasses. The former fields are defined by large and small ditches or channels that flow into the Combahee River through the primary canals that parallel the U.S. Highway 17 causeway.

  • 1700

    Rice becomes the most valuable crop in the new Carolina Colony.

  • 1790

    Planters begin to use the force of tides to control water in their rice fields.

  • 1850

    5.5 percent of the state's rice crop is produced along the Combahee River.
